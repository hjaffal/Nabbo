# Core Product Loop

## The Loop

```
Capture → Understand → Verify → Assign → Execute → Learn
```

Each stage must reduce mental load. If a stage adds work without creating clear value, it is broken.

---

## Stage 1: Capture

**Trigger:** The parent receives a family signal (WhatsApp message, email, screenshot, PDF, image, voice note, school app message, copied text, or quick reminder).

**Paths:**
1. Mobile share from iOS/Android
2. Email forwarding to family Nabbo alias
3. Free text or voice inside the app

**Success:** Sending something to Nabbo feels easier than remembering it.
**Failure:** The parent delays, forgets, or avoids using Nabbo because input feels too heavy.

---

## Stage 2: Understand

After capture, Nabbo reads the input and extracts **operational meaning** — not just a summary.

It detects: affected family member, event, task, deadline, time, date, location, required items, form, payment, owner, urgency, change, and uncertainty.

**Example:** A school trip email is not just an event. It may contain a date, departure time, return time, location, payment deadline, permission form, packed lunch requirement, clothing instruction, and parent action.

**Success:** The system extracts the useful parts without forcing the parent to reread and process everything manually.
**Failure:** The system produces vague summaries, misses action items, guesses too much, or treats every message as a calendar event.

---

## Stage 3: Verify

Nabbo does **not** push extracted information directly into the household plan without parent review.

The parent sees a Review Card showing what Nabbo found:
- Original source remains visible
- Extracted fields are clear
- Uncertain fields are highlighted
- Suggestions are separated from facts

Actions: approve, edit, dismiss, snooze, assign, or mark as already handled.

**Success:** The parent checks and approves an item quickly without doing the full mental work again.
**Failure:** The parent has to reread the whole message every time, correct too many fields, or loses confidence in extraction quality.

---

## Stage 4: Assign

Once verified, every action should either have an owner or be marked as unassigned.

A family task without an owner is not handled — it simply becomes invisible pressure.

Examples: pickup ownership, payment ownership, form ownership, packing ownership, follow-up ownership.

**Success:** Nabbo makes responsibility clear before the task becomes a problem.
**Failure:** Actions sit in the system without ownership, creating another passive list.

---

## Stage 5: Execute

The product shows what needs to happen now, today, tomorrow, and this week. It does not behave like a passive calendar.

The Today Command Center answers:
- What is next?
- What needs attention now?
- What must be packed?
- What is due today?
- What changed?
- What has no owner?
- What is at risk?

Includes: reminders, preparation checklists, deadline alerts, change alerts, ownership gaps, and daily/weekly briefs.

**Success:** The parent opens Nabbo during real pressure moments because it tells them what to do next.
**Failure:** Nabbo becomes another place to check, clean, or maintain.

---

## Stage 6: Learn

Nabbo becomes more useful over time by learning household patterns:
- Family members, schools, clubs, activities
- Common locations and repeated routines
- Usual required items and responsibility patterns

**Example:** Nabbo learns that swimming requires goggles and a towel, football happens on Tuesday, one parent usually handles activity payments.

Learning appears as useful suggestions, not intrusive automation.

**Success:** Nabbo reduces repeated thinking.
**Failure:** The system makes assumptions too aggressively, feels creepy, or suggests irrelevant actions.

---

## Loop Output

A captured message can become one or more of:

- Event
- Task
- Deadline
- Required item
- Checklist
- Reminder
- Payment action
- Form action
- Location
- Change alert
- Risk alert
- Owner assignment
- Weekly brief item

**Nabbo is not event-first. Nabbo is action-first.**

---

## Example Loop in Action

**Input:** WhatsApp message from a coach:
> "Training moved to Friday 18:30 at the Sports Hall. Please bring water, blue jersey, and size 4 ball."

**Parent shares to Nabbo.**

**Nabbo extracts:**
- Child affected: Adam
- Event: Football training
- Date: Friday
- Time: 18:30
- Location: Sports Hall
- Required items: water, blue jersey, size 4 ball
- Change detected: training time changed
- Action needed: pack items before departure
- Owner: unassigned

**Parent verifies and approves.**

**Nabbo updates Today:**
> "Football training at 18:30. Leave by 18:00. Bring water, blue jersey, and size 4 ball. Packing has no owner."

That is the product loop working.

---

## The Habit Test

The most important question:

> When a parent receives a school email, WhatsApp update, screenshot, form, or reminder, do they naturally think "I should Nabbo this"?

If yes → the product has a chance.
If no → the product is just another productivity app.

---

## Success Metrics

| Signal | What It Measures |
|--------|-----------------|
| Weekly captures per active household | Loop is alive |
| Capture speed | Friction level |
| Verification trust (items approved without major correction) | AI quality |
| Execution usage (Today opened during pressure moments) | Daily value |
| Ownership clarity (actions don't remain unassigned) | Responsibility model |
| Repeat use after novelty fades | Habit formation |

---

## Loop Risks

1. **Capture friction** — Nabbo is slower than remembering
2. **Weak extraction** — Nabbo summarizes but doesn't create useful actions
3. **False confidence** — Nabbo hides uncertainty, makes trust-breaking mistakes
4. **Passive storage** — Nabbo becomes a nicer inbox or calendar
5. **Missing ownership** — Actions have no owner, no household execution
6. **Over-notification** — Nabbo alerts too often, becomes noise
7. **Over-learning** — Nabbo assumes too much too early, feels unreliable or intrusive

---

## Product Rules

- Make capture easier than memory
- Convert information into action
- Show uncertainty clearly
- Keep the original source visible
- Require verification before important actions enter the plan
- Make ownership visible
- Highlight changes and risks
- Avoid becoming a chat app, calendar clone, or generic task manager
- Reduce repeated thinking over time
